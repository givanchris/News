## Top Stories

### 1. Oil Holds Above $100 as Trump Threatens to "Obliterate" Iran's Energy Infrastructure
WTI crude settled at $102.88/bbl (+3.25%), and Brent is on track for a record monthly gain of approximately 55% — the largest in the contract's history, surpassing the 46% surge during the Gulf War in September 1990. The Iran war and its associated closure of the Strait of Hormuz have disrupted an estimated 20% of global oil supply and significant LNG volumes. President Trump on Monday threatened to destroy Iran's energy infrastructure if a diplomatic deal is not reached quickly, adding a further escalation premium to an already elevated market. The macroeconomic spillovers are significant: U.S. import prices ran +1.3% month-over-month in February (the largest since March 2022), and gasoline at the pump has risen above $4/gallon nationally. Energy industry executives warn that economic damage escalates sharply if the Strait remains disrupted beyond one to three more weeks. ### 2. Fed Holds at 3.50–3.75%, Powell Comfortable "Waiting to See"
The Federal Reserve held rates unchanged at its March meeting — its second consecutive hold following three quarter-point cuts in late 2025 — with Chair Powell stating rates are "in a good place" given the current environment. The Fed explicitly acknowledged that the Iran conflict has materially complicated the dual-mandate calculus: higher oil prices push inflation up while simultaneously threatening growth, leaving the Fed caught in a classic stagflationary bind. Critically, last week short-term yields briefly spiked — the 2-year and 3-year Treasury yields flipped to price in rate *hikes*, temporarily uninverting the curve in a hawkish direction, and the government auctioned $606 billion of securities in a single week. Markets are now pricing a non-trivial probability of a rate increase later in 2026 if energy prices remain elevated. The Fed's next meeting concludes April 29. ### 3. ECB Also on Hold at 2.0%, Warns of "Significantly More Uncertain" Outlook
The European Central Bank held its deposit rate at 2.0% at its March 19 meeting, citing the Iran war as having created "upside risks for inflation and downside risks for economic growth" — the standard stagflation framing now being used by every major central bank. The ECB revised its 2026 headline inflation forecast up to 2.6% (from prior guidance near 2%), attributing the overshoot entirely to energy. Critically, euro area GDP growth is now projected at just 0.8% in 2026 — a razor-thin margin above contraction that reflects the continent's far greater energy import dependence relative to the U.S. The ECB's rate-cutting cycle, which was consensus as recently as January, is now effectively frozen. Any further energy price escalation or a Hormuz closure extension could force a policy reversal that markets are only beginning to price. ### 4. OECD Cuts Global Growth Forecasts, Raises Inflation 1.2 pp — Stagflation Framework Now Official
The OECD's March 2026 Interim Outlook raised its G20 inflation forecast by 1.2 percentage points to 4.0% for 2026 while cutting global GDP growth to 2.9%, with the divergence squarely attributed to the Middle East energy shock. U.S. growth is now projected at 2.0% for 2026 (falling to 1.7% in 2027); the euro area at 0.8% in 2026; and China at 4.4%. The OECD explicitly flagged that "prolonged disruptions to Middle East exports could produce a more marked price reaction and aggravate shortages of key commodities," with downstream risks including broader financial market repricing, weakening private demand, and financial stability concerns. This is the institutional macro establishment formally endorsing what the bond and equity markets have been pricing since early March — that the supply shock has materially worsened the growth/inflation tradeoff for policymakers. ### 5. Micron Down 30% Since Earnings: The "Sell the News + Structural Fear" Double Hit
Micron reported extraordinary Q2 results on March 18 — revenue of $23.86 billion (+196% year-over-year) and non-GAAP EPS of $12.20 against a consensus of ~$9.30 (+680% YoY) — yet the stock has fallen roughly 30% since the print. Today's 10% decline extended the streak to eight consecutive down sessions. Two forces are driving the sell-off: first, management raised FY2026 capex guidance to over $25 billion (up from $20 billion), which markets interpret as a warning about capital intensity at the peak of the cycle; second, Google's TurboQuant announcement (see below) has raised structural questions about the long-term AI memory demand trajectory. The setup is a useful illustration of how "beat-and-raise" can fail when the narrative beneath the numbers shifts — investors are discounting peak-cycle earnings and a potential future where efficiency gains erode the memory supercycle thesis. Summit Insights downgraded Micron from Buy to Hold post-earnings, though 38 of 43 covering analysts still rate it a Buy. ### 6. Google's TurboQuant: 6x AI Memory Compression Rattles the Semiconductor Complex
Google Research published TurboQuant, a compression algorithm that reduces KV-cache memory consumption by 6x — compressing each KV-cache value from 16 bits to 3 bits — with zero accuracy loss and no model retraining required. On Nvidia H100 GPUs, it achieves up to 8x faster inference throughput and reportedly cuts memory-related costs by 50% or more. The immediate market reaction was severe: SK Hynix fell ~6%, Samsung ~5%, Kioxia ~6%, and Micron continued its post-earnings slide. The bear case is straightforward — if each model runs on one-sixth the memory, forward demand projections for HBM and DRAM may be too high. The bull counterargument invokes Jevons Paradox: lower cost-per-inference historically leads to far higher utilization, with developers filling the freed capacity with more ambitious applications, ultimately increasing total memory consumption. TurboQuant's formal technical presentation is slated for ICLR 2026 in late April, which will be the next inflection point for this narrative. The modest flight-to-safety bid reflects genuine recession concern, but it is in direct tension with the inflation risk premium from sustained $100+ oil. Last week, 2- and 3-year yields briefly inverted upward (suggesting markets priced a rate hike ahead of longer maturities), then reversed — illustrating acute uncertainty about whether the Fed's next move is a cut or a hike. The government sold $606 billion of Treasury securities in a single week, underscoring ongoing fiscal supply pressure that limits how far yields can fall even in a growth scare. The bond market is effectively acting as a real-time referendum on the stagflation debate, and it has not reached a verdict.
