### 2. Oil's Historic March: Brent Up 63%, WTI Up 48% — Largest Monthly Surge Since 1988
Brent crude posted its largest single-month percentage gain in nearly four decades, closing March around $107–108, driven entirely by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz on March 2. Approximately 17.8 million barrels per day — roughly one-fifth of global crude flows — have been disrupted. Iran has introduced a yuan-denominated "toll booth" system allowing select Chinese, Russian, and allied tankers to transit, further fragmenting the global oil market. WTI settled around $101–103. The April 6 Trump deadline — threatening "severe consequences" if Hormuz remains closed — is the single most important binary event in commodities markets right now. If no deal materializes, another leg higher in energy prices is likely, with direct pass-through into headline CPI and PCE. ### 3. February PCE Inflation: Core Holds at ~3.0%, Giving Fed Breathing Room — For Now
February core PCE inflation came in at approximately 3.0% year-over-year (January was 3.06%), remaining stubbornly above the Fed's 2% target but below the 3.5%+ prints that would force an emergency response. The 10-year Treasury rallied off its morning high of 4.31% to 4.22% after the release, suggesting the print was roughly in-line with expectations. The critical complexity for policymakers: energy-driven headline inflation is structurally elevated by the Hormuz closure, but core remains a separate regime — for now. If energy costs persist, second-round effects through transportation, logistics, and manufacturing will eventually bleed into core. The Fed is threading a needle between an oil-shock-driven inflation spike and a demand-driven growth slowdown. ### 4. Fed Holds at 3.50–3.75%; Stagflation Trap Sets Up as the Central Policy Dilemma of 2026
The Federal Reserve held rates unchanged at its March 17–18 meeting, choosing to wait rather than respond to what it characterized as supply-shock inflation. The problem is now acute: oil at $100+ is a tax on consumers and businesses, compressing real incomes and profit margins, while simultaneously pushing headline inflation higher. Cutting rates to support growth risks entrenching energy-driven inflation; holding or hiking risks tipping a slowing economy into recession. Markets are currently pricing modest rate cuts later in the year — a bet that growth deterioration wins the argument over inflation. That view could be tested sharply if the Hormuz closure extends into Q2. ### 5. ECB Holds at 2.0%, Flags "Significantly More Uncertain" Outlook; Revises Inflation Upward
The ECB kept its deposit rate at 2.0% at its March 19 meeting and revised its 2026 inflation forecast up to 2.6% (from prior estimates), explicitly attributing the revision to energy price pass-through from the Iran conflict. This is a notable hawkish signal: the ECB had been on a normalization path and is now frozen by an external supply shock. European economies are disproportionately exposed to Middle East energy disruptions compared to the U.S. — the eurozone imports roughly 90% of its natural gas needs and is acutely vulnerable to any further escalation. EUR/USD dynamics will be heavily driven by relative central bank divergence expectations in Q2. ### 7. McCormick to Acquire Unilever's Global Foods Division in $44.8B Reverse Morris Trust
McCormick & Company announced a definitive agreement to acquire Unilever's global foods division — including Hellmann's, Knorr, and other major brands — in a $44.8 billion Reverse Morris Trust transaction. The structure involves roughly $15.7 billion in cash plus McCormick equity, with Unilever shareholders expected to hold ~65% of the combined company at closing. McCormick's own Q3 results were strong: adjusted EPS of $0.66 vs. $0.60 expected, and net revenues up 16.7% YoY. Shares rallied ~5.5% on the announcement. The deal would create one of the largest global condiment and flavor companies. The Reverse Morris Trust structure is notable — it allows Unilever to divest the unit in a tax-efficient manner by spinning it to shareholders and simultaneously merging it with McCormick, subject to IRS approval requirements. ### 8. Treasury Market Volatility: 10Y Briefly Touched 3.92% on Recession Fears, Bouncing to 4.22%
The 10-year Treasury yield fell as low as 3.92% on March 30 as a "flight to quality" trade overwhelmed inflation fears — a stark signal that recession risk is now being priced alongside elevated energy costs. The subsequent partial rebound to 4.22% reflects the market's struggle to price a genuine stagflationary scenario: bonds rally on growth fears, then sell off when inflation persistence reasserts itself. This volatile rate backdrop creates real problems for corporate credit: investment-grade spreads are widening as borrowers face both elevated base rates and deteriorating growth expectations, while high-yield faces the additional headwind of margin compression from energy costs. ### 9. Gold's Worst March Since 2008: Real Yield Shock Overwhelms Safe-Haven Demand
Gold fell sharply in March despite a textbook safe-haven catalyst (geopolitical conflict, equity volatility), closing around $4,677/oz and posting its worst monthly performance since 2008. The driver is counterintuitive but analytically important: real yields — nominal Treasury yields minus inflation expectations — moved higher as the market priced oil-shock inflation as persistent. Higher real yields raise the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold. The dollar also strengthened meaningfully (DXY +~2% MTD), adding a second headwind. This dynamic illustrates why "geopolitical risk" alone is insufficient to predict gold's direction — the asset is ultimately priced off real yields and dollar strength, both of which moved against it even as risk events multiplied. ### 10. April 6 Hormuz Deadline: The Binary Event That Will Define Q2 Market Direction
With Brent up 63% in March and the Strait of Hormuz closed since March 2, the Trump administration has set April 6 as the deadline for Iran to reopen the waterway or face "severe consequences." Two starkly different outcomes exist: a negotiated ceasefire (similar to today's rally), which would rapidly unwind energy prices and support risk assets; or continued blockade/escalation, which would extend supply disruption and potentially push Brent toward $130+, threatening stagflation, further central bank paralysis, and a meaningful growth slowdown across energy-importing economies. Asian economies — particularly India and South Korea — are most acutely exposed with import cover of only 33–60 days. This single geopolitical variable is currently the primary driver of macro outcomes for Q2 2026. ## What to Watch

- **April 6 Hormuz Deadline**: The most important binary event in markets — Trump's ultimatum to Iran expires. A deal vs. escalation will define commodity prices, inflation expectations, and central bank policy paths heading into Q2.
- **ISM Manufacturing PMI (April 1)**: First look at how manufacturers are absorbing the oil shock. Input cost components and new orders are the key sub-indices to watch — any contraction below 50 alongside elevated price indices would be a clear stagflationary signal.
- **Fed Communication**: Watch for Fed speakers interpreting today's PCE print in light of energy prices. Any hints that the Fed would look through oil-driven inflation (or wouldn't) will reprice the rate cut timeline materially.
- **Nike / China Consumer Sentiment**: Nike's 20% China decline guidance reinforces a broader theme of softening Chinese consumer spending — relevant not just for consumer brands but as a leading indicator of global trade and manufacturing demand.
- **Q1 2026 GDP Tracking**: Atlanta Fed GDPNow and similar real-time trackers will be updated this week incorporating PCE data. Given the oil price surge, any meaningful Q1 GDP downgrade would intensify recession-vs-stagflation debate at the Fed.
